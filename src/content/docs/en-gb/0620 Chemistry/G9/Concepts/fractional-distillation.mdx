Fractional distillation is used when the boiling points of the liquids are too close for effective separation by simple distillation. It achieves better separation through repeated vaporization and condensation cycles.

## Key Principle

A fractionating column provides a large surface area for repeated equilibration between vapor and liquid phases, allowing components to be separated according to their boiling points in a stepwise manner.

## Required Apparatus

- All items required for simple distillation, plus:
- Fractionating column (e.g., packed with glass beads, Raschig rings, or Vigreux indentations)
- Distillation flask
- Anti-bumping granules
- Thermometer
- Condenser
- Receiver adapter
- Multiple receiving vessels (to collect different fractions)

## Procedure

1. Place the mixture and anti-bumping granules into the distillation flask.
2. Attach the fractionating column to the top of the distillation flask.
3. Connect the thermometer, condenser, and receiver adapter as in simple distillation.
4. Run cold water through the condenser.
5. Heat the mixture gently and steadily.
6. Collect fractions when the temperature stabilizes at different plateaus (each corresponding to a component’s boiling point).
7. Change the receiving vessel when the temperature begins to rise toward the next component’s boiling point.
8. Record the boiling range and appearance of each fraction.
9. Stop heating once all desired fractions are collected and allow the apparatus to cool.

## Important Notes

- The fractionating column should be insulated if high efficiency is required.
- Slower heating improves separation by allowing more equilibration steps within the column.
- This technique is commonly used in the separation of crude oil fractions, alcohol-water mixtures, and other close-boiling liquid systems.
