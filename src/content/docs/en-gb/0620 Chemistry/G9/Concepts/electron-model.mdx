## Core Idea

Most chemical properties can be understood using one simple idea:

> Atoms differ in how strongly they attract electrons

This affects:

- Whether an element is a metal or non-metal
- How reactive it is
- Trends in the periodic table

## 1. Metals vs Non-metals

### Metals

- Tend to **lose electrons**
- Form positive ions (cations)

👉 Result:

- Good conductors
- Malleable and ductile

---

### Non-metals

- Tend to **gain electrons**
- Form negative ions (anions)

👉 Result:

- Poor conductors
- Often brittle (if solid)

## 2. Periodic Trends

---

### Across a Period

- Proton number increases
- Attraction to electrons increases

👉 Result:

- Less likely to lose electrons
- More likely to gain electrons
- Properties change from **metal → non-metal**

---

### Down a Group

- More electron shells
- Increased shielding effect

👉 Result:

- Outer electrons are less strongly held

## 3. Reactivity

---

### Metals

- More reactive → lose electrons more easily

👉 Example:

- Group I becomes more reactive down the group

---

### Non-metals

- More reactive → gain electrons more easily

👉 Example:

- Group VII becomes less reactive down the group

## 4. A Simple Model

You can think of atoms as competing for electrons:

- Metals: weak hold on electrons
- Non-metals: strong attraction for electrons

## 5. How to Use This Model

When solving problems, ask:

> Does this atom tend to lose or gain electrons?

This helps explain:

- Reactivity trends
- Displacement reactions
- Position in the periodic table

## 6. Going Further

This idea connects to:

- Effective nuclear charge
- Electronegativity

At this level, you only need to remember:

> The ability of an atom to attract electrons determines its behaviour

## One-line Summary

> Chemical reactions involve the transfer or sharing of electrons
