Paper chromatography separates substances according to their relative solubility in a solvent (mobile phase) and affinity for the paper (stationary phase).

## Key Terms

- **Mobile phase**: solvent (e.g. water)
- **Stationary phase**: chromatography paper
- **Baseline**: starting line where spots are placed
- **Solvent front**: furthest distance reached by the solvent
- **Locating agent**: chemical used to visualize colorless spots

## Principle

More soluble components travel faster with the solvent; less soluble or more strongly adsorbed components move slowly or remain near the baseline.

## Procedure

1. Draw a pencil baseline ~2 cm from the bottom edge.
2. Place small spots of samples on the baseline, spaced apart.
3. Pour solvent into a beaker (depth < baseline height).
4. Suspend paper so only the bottom edge touches the solvent.
5. Allow solvent to rise about halfway up the paper.
6. Remove paper, immediately mark the solvent front.
7. Dry and observe / apply locating agent if necessary.

## Rf Value

Rf = (distance travelled by substance) / (distance travelled by solvent front)

Rf values are constant for a given compound under identical conditions and are used for identification.

**Important notes**

- Always use pencil for baseline (ink would dissolve).
- Remove paper before solvent reaches the top to prevent loss of spots.
